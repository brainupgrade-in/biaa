Bridge from Day 1

Images were easy — language is a sequence

Day 1’s networks took a fixed-size input (784 pixels) and produced one answer. Language is different: a variable-length sequence where meaning depends on order and on far-apart context.

🎨 An image

  • Fixed size, all pixels available at once
  • A plain neural net handles it well (Day 1)
  • No notion of “order” to preserve

💬 A sentence

  • Variable length, arrives as a sequence
  • Order flips meaning: “dog bites man” ≠ “man bites dog”
  • Context can be far away: a pronoun and its noun

💡 But a neural network reads numbers, not words — so step one is turning language into numbers. Then we can ask how to model the sequence: RNNs → the transformer.

Two problems that held RNNs back

🧬 1 · Long-range memory fades

By the end of a long sentence, early words are nearly forgotten. The model struggles to link a pronoun to a noun far away.

🐢 2 · No parallelism

Step N needs step N−1’s memory, so words must be processed strictly in order — you can’t use a GPU’s thousands of cores at once. Training is slow and hard to scale.

⚡ The transformer (2017) fixes both at once: it sees every word simultaneously (parallel) and lets any word look directly at any other (no fading). The mechanism that does this is attention.

The breakthrough

Attention — look at everything, weigh what matters

Instead of a single running memory, attention lets each word look directly at every other word at the same time, and decide how much each one matters right now — a weight per word.

👀 Look at all

Every word can see every other word in the sentence — at once, no fading.

⚖️ Weigh relevance

Assign each a weight: how relevant is that word to understanding this one?

➕ Blend

Mix the others’ meaning by those weights to enrich this word’s representation.

📄 The 2017 paper that introduced this was titled “Attention Is All You Need” — it removed recurrence entirely and kept only attention. That architecture is the transformer.

Self-attention · a concrete example▶ HANDS-ON · Labs 08–09

“it” learns to look at “animal”

You already do this. Reading “…because it was tired,” your mind jumps straight back to animal — not street — with zero effort. Self-attention gives a model the same reflex: for every word, glance across the whole sentence and decide which words actually explain it.

💡 Everyday parallel — the cocktail-party effect: in a loud room you lock onto the one voice that matters and let the rest fade to a murmur. Attention is that spotlight inside a sentence — for each word it turns up the few words that are relevant and turns the others down.  ▶ Play with the spotlight

Inside attention · the Q/K/V recipe▶ HANDS-ON · Lab 03

Self-attention: Query, Key, Value

So how does a word decide what to look at? Under the hood, each word runs a tiny search against every other word — using three roles you meet every time you look something up.

🔍 Everyday parallel — searching a video site: your Query is what you type; each video’s Key is its title & tags (how it advertises itself); the Value is the video you actually watch. Attention just does it softly — instead of one result, it blends a weighted mix of every Value.

Attention is order-blind · put order back▶ HANDS-ON · Lab 04

Positional encoding — putting order back in

Attention sees the words as a set, with no inherent order. So we add a position signal to each token’s embedding — now the model knows word 1 from word 5, and “dog bites man” differs from “man bites dog.”

💡 Without this, a transformer couldn’t tell word order at all. Adding position is what lets a parallel, order-blind mechanism still understand sequence.

The family split · BERT vs GPT▶ HANDS-ON · Labs 05 & 10

Encoder vs decoder — understanding vs generating

📚 Encoder — BERT-style

Reads the whole input at once, each word seeing both directions. Builds deep understanding.

  • Best at understanding: classification, sentiment, search, NER
  • Family: BERT, DistilBERT, RoBERTa
  • → Your BERT sentiment fine-tuning lab (Module 4)

✏️ Decoder — GPT-style

Generates text left to right, each new word seeing only the words before it. Predicts the next token.

  • Best at generating: writing, chat, code, summarisation
  • Family: GPT, Llama, Mistral, Gemma
  • → Your GPT text-generation lab (Module 3)

🧠 Same building block, two uses. Encoders understand (is this review positive?); decoders generate (write the reply). LLMs & agents are mostly decoders; many classifiers are encoders.

You load one — you don’t build it▶ HANDS-ON · Lab 11

A transformer in a few lines (Hugging Face)

# DECODER (GPT-style) -- generate text from transformers import pipeline gen = pipeline("text-generation", model="distilgpt2") out = gen("Agentic AI is", max_new_tokens=20) # "Agentic AI is a way to let models # take actions toward a goal ..."
# ENCODER (BERT-style) -- understand text from transformers import pipeline clf = pipeline("sentiment-analysis") clf("This workshop is fantastic!") # [{'label': 'POSITIVE', # 'score': 0.9998}]

👉 A pipeline hides tokenization + the model + decoding. Your labs run these on small, free, local models — no API key. The OpenAI GPT API is the same idea, just a paid, hosted decoder.

Recap

Five things to carry forward

1 · Language is a sequence

Order & far-apart context matter — plain nets & RNNs struggled with both.

2 · Attention is the idea

Every word looks at every other word and weighs what’s relevant — no fading, all at once.

Self-attention = match a Query to Keys, blend the Values by the resulting weights.

Quick check · Q1 / 10

Why did RNNs struggle with long sequences?

AThey used too little memory
BThey had no trainable weights
CThey were too parallel
DThey process tokens one at a time and ‘forget’ distant context (vanishing gradients)
Answer · Q1 / 10

Sequential & forgetful

The bottleneck transformers removed.

AThey used too little memory
BThey had no trainable weights
CThey were too parallel
DThey process tokens one at a time and ‘forget’ distant context (vanishing gradients) ✓

WHY  RNNs read sequentially and their signal decays over distance (vanishing gradients), so early context is lost and they can't be parallelised.

AConvolution over pixels
BDeeper recurrence
CAttention — every token can look at every other token
DBigger images
Answer · Q2 / 10

Attention

‘Attention is all you need.’

AConvolution over pixels
BDeeper recurrence
CAttention — every token can look at every other token ✓
DBigger images

WHY  Attention lets every token directly weigh every other token — no recurrence, fully parallel. That single idea (2017) underpins all modern LLMs.

Quick check · Q3 / 10

Self-attention works with three vectors per token, called…

AQuery, Key, Value (Q, K, V)
BRed, Green, Blue
CInput, Output, Hidden
DTrain, Test, Validate
Answer · Q3 / 10

Query, Key, Value

The three roles each token plays.

AQuery, Key, Value (Q, K, V) ✓
BRed, Green, Blue
CInput, Output, Hidden
DTrain, Test, Validate

WHY  Each token has a Query, a Key and a Value. A token's Query is compared to every Key to get weights, which blend the Values into its new representation.

Quick check · Q4 / 10

A big reason transformers beat RNNs at scale is that attention is…

ASlower but simpler
BParallelisable — all positions computed at once, not step-by-step
CPurely rule-based
DUnable to run on GPUs
Answer · Q4 / 10

Parallel training

Why they train on huge data.

ASlower but simpler
BParallelisable — all positions computed at once, not step-by-step ✓
CPurely rule-based
DUnable to run on GPUs

WHY  With no recurrence, a transformer processes the whole sequence at once — perfect for GPUs. That parallelism is what let models scale to billions of parameters.

Why do transformers need positional encoding?

BTo compress the model
CTo label the data

Attention ignores order

Putting word order back.

AAttention itself is order-agnostic, so position must be added back in ✓
BTo compress the model
CTo label the data

WHY  Attention sees a set, not a sequence — ‘dog bites man’ = ‘man bites dog’ without help. Positional encoding adds order back into the embeddings.

Quick check · Q7 / 10

Roughly, attention makes a token’s new representation…

AA copy of the first token
BA random vector
CUnchanged from its input
DA weighted blend of the other tokens’ values, weighted by relevance
Answer · Q7 / 10

A weighted blend

Context, mixed in by relevance.

AA copy of the first token
BA random vector
CUnchanged from its input
DA weighted blend of the other tokens’ values, weighted by relevance ✓

WHY  A token becomes a relevance-weighted mix of the others — so ‘bank’ near ‘river’ ends up different from ‘bank’ near ‘money’. That's contextual meaning.

Quick check · Q8 / 10

GPT-style (decoder-only) models generate text by…

AReading the whole future at once
BSorting words alphabetically
CPredicting the next token, one at a time, from the tokens so far
DTranslating text into images
Answer · Q8 / 10

Next-token prediction

How a generative LLM writes.

AReading the whole future at once
BSorting words alphabetically
CPredicting the next token, one at a time, from the tokens so far ✓
DTranslating text into images

WHY  A decoder-only model is a next-token predictor: given the tokens so far, output the most likely next one, append it, repeat. That loop is text generation.
